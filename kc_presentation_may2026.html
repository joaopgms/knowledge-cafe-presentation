the problem

kids grow up online.
nobody teaches them how to read what they see.

Young people today spend hours online with very little preparation for what they encounter. Reading a manipulated image, spotting a misleading headline, tracing a source back to its origin: these are skills that schools and families rarely teach.

the bubbles

Algorithms do not show the world.
They show a version of the world calibrated to hold your attention.

People who grow up inside a bubble often have no idea the bubble exists. This shapes how young people understand politics, society, and each other.

what this design does to society

the cost is bigger
than we talk about.

political polarisation

Algorithms reward outrage. People are pushed toward the extreme of their own side, with no exposure to anything outside it.

shrinking attention spans

Growing difficulty reading long texts, sustaining focus, tolerating boredom. Measurable in young adults who grew up with smartphones.

collapse of trust in institutions

Repeated misinformation creates doubt in media, science and government. The information ecosystem makes everything feel unreliable.

coordinated electoral manipulation

Deliberate campaigns that exploit algorithmic amplification to spread false narratives at critical moments in democratic processes.

adolescent mental health crisis

Documented correlation between heavy social media use and anxiety, depression and body image disorders, especially in girls aged 11 to 16.

problem. gap. solution.

control isn't enough.

the instinct

Control.

Screen time limits. Parental control apps. It can help. But it doesn't fix the problem.

the real gap

Nothing to think with.

We've restricted access. We haven't built capacity.

the real solution

Empowerment.

Identify risks. Question what they see. Recognise a nudge. Think, sometimes, away from screens.

project I

Navi.

my personal market analyst.

A Flask app with a Gemini 3.1 agent that pulls live prices, searches news, and returns BUY/HOLD/SELL with reasoning. It emails me at 7am. It scores risk. It logs every decision. The kind of tool that used to require a team.

An Analyst rewrites the strategy every morning. A Scout researches the slate. A Commit agent acts 75 minutes before tip-off. A Settler reconciles overnight. Different models, different roles, one shared playbook the agents edit themselves.

project III

OML_XML.

the boring win. The one I use every week.

OutSystems modules ship as opaque .oml binaries. Reading them used to mean hex editors and guesswork. With AI I built a tool that converts any module into clean XML and a Markdown report in seconds. Unglamorous. Indispensable.

project IV

SSL pinning.

the mobile cert lockdown.

Pinned all eight Amazon Trust Services root CAs in the OutSystems mobile app for salland-dev.outsystems.app. A Node script re-checks live cert hashes against the pinned set. Four-stage test suite: baseline, tampered hashes, MITM via mitmproxy, cert rotation. All passed. The mitmproxy test is the proof — the app refused to talk to the attacker.

project V

sallanddoc.

a skill that documents an entire OutSystems app for me.

I packaged everything I learned about Mijn Salland — ODC architecture, the wrapper pattern, OHI REST paths, Confluence rules, JQL ticket search — into a custom Claude skill. Every new feature doc now starts with the skill loaded. Three canonical pages already live on the SMO Confluence space. The agent searches Jira, drafts the page, and publishes it. I just provide screenshots and mentor analysis.

project VI

SallandFactoryAnalysis.

the audit I would never have finished by hand.

The Salland ODC factory has fifty assets and 545 Application Objects — over our subscription limit. I had AI inventory every asset, classify each by AO tier, and produce a static-entity analysis for every major module. The path to compliance is now visible.
